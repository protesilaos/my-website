---
title: "Religion and theology in light of the Greek gods"
excerpt: "Extended commentary on how I understand matters of ethnicity, religion, and theology."
---

A friend of mine told me about this CNN article which covers the
religious practices of a polytheistic group in Athens. [These people
say worshiping ancient Greek gods is a route to modern happiness](https://edition.cnn.com/travel/athens-worshiping-ancient-greek-gods-labrys).
There are many such groups, including in Cyprus where I live. The news
story gives me the pretext to comment on some related topics that I
have already covered in one way or the other in previous publications.

First off, they make a reference to the latest film about the Odyssey.
I have expounded on my opinion [about the absence of Greeks from
Hollywood's Odyssey movie](https://protesilaos.com/politics/2026-06-03-absence-greeks-hollywood-odyssey-movie/)
(2026-06-03). The gist is to (i) understand art as symbolic so that
you are not an idolater in practice, (ii) ignore Hollywood because the
kinds of people it promotes are typically corrupt, and (iii) the
absence or presence of Greeks is a distraction.

Now to the topic of religion and theology. Theology is a branch of
philosophy. It is what we do to understand what might be the divine.
Theology necessarily overlaps with metaphysics, for it pertains to the
abstract structure of all that is. Or, to put it differently, it wants
to understand what is the pattern or reason by which the world of
physics unfolds.

To me, "theology" and "metaphysics" are two words for the same study,
though I am content with keeping them conceptually distinct in the
interest of communicating different types of emphasis. For example,
theology focuses on god while metaphysics is concerned with abstract
structure without mentioning god. Though even writing this out makes
me notice the arbitrariness of the distinction, but whatever.

What theology/metaphysics does is discern the common in the multitude
of all phenomena as that which is both a precondition for them as well
as the irreducible factor in their manifestation. In the Greek
tradition, god is Logos (Λόγος): that which transcends and permeates
all. It is neither human-like, nor agentic, nor disposed a certain way
(e.g. as benevolent sky father). Logos literally means all of the
following, which we commonly employ in everyday Greek parlance:

- reason (the cause of something);
- reason (as in reasonableness);
- speech (and by extension language);
- ratio (and by extension pattern or structure).

Plus there are other common uses, such as the suffix "-logy" that we
find in the English name of various fields of study. Those
applications are consistent with the aforementioned.

Presence has pattern. It has ratio, it forms structure. It unfolds
through cause and effect in what effectively is a feedback loop that
is indistinguishable from communication and thus language.

Logos is constant. It is. We may say "it always is" for effect, though
we do not imply temporality or locality: there is no before or after
it, nor inside and outside of it. It is total and complete in its
oneness.

Hesiod, an ancient poet, claims that in the beginning there was Chaos.
Strictly speaking, chaos refers to the absence of structure (so "chaos
theory" in mathematics is not the same thing because it still is about
pattern, for it discerns something to be studied and reasoned about,
hence theory). A primordial Chaos is the kind of careless claim that
philosophers must remind poets not to mention in passing. If something
comes from a putative patternlessness, then that has had in it the
capacity for cause and effect, else the capacity for pattern and
structure, and, therefore, it was never patternlessness per se.

To even describe something as "patternless" necessarily requires that
it exists as patternless, in the state of patternlessness, and is
identifiable as such. To identify is to discern, to discern is to
trace pattern, to trace pattern is, in short, to recognise an
instantiation of Logos. Primordial Chaos is useful only in a loose
metaphorical sense. But even then we can think of more apt metaphors
(and, by the way, Hesiod does a terrible job with some of his
metaphors that debase the gods in what effectively is ancient
pornography).

Which brings me to the topic of art and mythology. Theology is for few
people. Not because we are elitist snobs, but due to the nature of the
topics involved. Few have the patience to follow along, let alone
meditate in isolation and build up the requisite capacity.

The Greek gods and all of mythology are human constructs, products of
Greek culture that reflect Greek values, all realised as Greek art. We
imagine them, depict them in certain ways, and tell stories that are
meant to teach something valuable to people.

We speak of many gods with the understanding that one and the many are
differences of focus or emphasis, not category, like the one ray of
light that is understood as many colours once it is seen through a
prism. The multitude of gods is a useful didactic and mnemonic device:
polytheism is a practical tool. We can capture phenomena in the world
and describe them in memorable ways.

For example, we can speak of Athena as the goddess of wisdom who is at
once wise and a formidable warrior. This representation triggers the
imagination and has people paying attention. The audience wants to
know why do we associate wisdom with (i) a woman and (ii) a warrior.
Why not have some male monk meditating in the mountains? Or some
pacifist librarian and scholar? Those have their purpose as well,
though we do it this way to communicate the following non-exhaustive
list of teachings, which we consider more important for social
reproduction:

- Females are not weak (and actually there are no weak goddesses in
  the 6 males and 6 females arrangement of the Olympians which
  represents the equality of the sexes through their innate
  differences).

- Power is not limited to raw physical prowess. The right attitude is
  mightier than the fist. As such, Athena's power consists in her
  perfect poise.

- Wisdom is about judgement and control. Even when you are committed
  to non-violence, you understand that sometimes you have to fight.
  "Sometimes" is where judicious reasonableness comes in.

- As a hoplite (infantry) bearing a shield and wielding a short-ranged
  weapon, Athena fights from the front lines in a phalanx formation.
  She thus is fearless to come forth (we have the virtue of parrhesia,
  after all, which is essential for philosophy---and I am doing now by
  publishing this otherwise unconventional piece) and will stand
  shoulder to shoulder with her peers, so she accepts the role of the
  one as part of the many. She is not self-obsessed and thus sees the
  bigger picture, which is what wisdom demands.

We can interpret all the gods in this way, but I will not belabour the
point.

The symbolism, then, is about reinforcing one's memory of the
teachings by fastening them upon the construct of an awesome figure
who has an interesting background and looks cool in what they do. Yes,
"cool" and "awesome" to get the people going.

People are inspired by excellence and grandeur. Just the other day the
world was admiring the accomplishments of elite footballers playing
for their national teams. Even in nominally monotheistic countries
there is endless demand for stories of heroes who do something
noteworthy, be they actual people or fictional characters. Go anywhere
you want and you will find millions of Lionel Messi and Cristiano
Ronaldo fans, for instance.

Just think of how many kids have been inspired by the likes of newer
starts, like Kylian Mbappe and Lamine Yamal, to commit to excellence
through the medium of sport. They do it because those footballers are
doing something admirable; something incredibly hard to achieve.

And even those who do not emulate their heroes one-to-one still find
elements of heroism that are applicable to their own life. Not
everyone has the unflinching commitment of an athlete, for example,
though everybody acknowledges that even a little bit of restraint and
discipline does them good.

Furthermore, the tool of polytheism is a way of recognising the
diversity among humans: how some are endowed with certain talents
while others have different ones. There is no better or worse god,
just as there is not one set of talents that is beneficial for
society: everybody counts. One person will feel closer to Athena, for
example, and another will relate more to Dionysos. In actuality, every
person has a little bit of every archetype. Together we form the
collective from which we all can benefit.

Philosophers can talk in abstract terms, but metaphors and such
mnemonics are helpful to everyone, especially those who are not
philosophising.

Which brings me to the problématique of what is religion. Unlike
theology, religion is for everyone. Its precepts must be practical so
that every member of society can apply them. Its lessons need to be
clear for each individual to feel involved. And its rites must
reinforce the sense of togetherness among the practitioners. To put it
succinctly, religion is a community equipped with a comphrehensive
framework for every facet of life.

Religion benefits from art and mythology because those are essential
to human's need for recreational activities. In Greek, we describe
"recreational activities" with a far more significant term:
psychagogia (ψυχαγωγία), which literally means "soul-teaching". As
such, we see in recreational activities the potential of growing as a
person and of expanding one's conscience beyond the day-to-day trivia.
Like with every teaching though, it cannot be all about studying: one
must also be practical, do some work, and generally proceed through
action. So psychagogia is not a license for laziness.

Religion, however, is not beholden to the artist in particular. Since
the CNN article mentions the Odyssey, I must stress that Homer is not
religion. He uses the gods as characters in his stories. The modern
equivalent would be some kind of superhero, saiyan, and the like.
Artists can interpret the gods, though they have to be aware that they
hold a special power for which they are responsible and accountable:
the power of formulating symbols.

As such, religion must have a self-contained traditionalism, which
strengthens the cohesion of its community over the aeons by providing
a bulwark against ill thought ideologies or trends of artistic
expression that come and go with the times.

Religion, then, is not a cult for hipsters. It has to stay in place
for the long-term. It is intergenerational in outlook and robust to
the vicissitudes of fashion. This means that at its core it has to
have a clear understanding of the family (which is the extended family
in the Greek tradition) and of social reproduction more broadly.
"Social reproduction" refers to biological continuity (i.e. children)
plus the regeneration of the cultural framing of the given society.
Everybody is welcome, yes, as all people matter, though the core
objective and ultimate value cannot deviate from the imperative of
survival through solidarity between generations.

In this sense, religion is ethnic (not to be confused with
nationalist, totalitarian, etc.): it encompasses the culture of a
specific people and it functions as the mechanism that keeps those
people around throughout the centuries. Religion that does not
recognise ethnicity will always degenerate into imperialism, which
will then engender materialism and inevitably greed (and even the
dominant religions of our planet which claim to be ecumenical still
have inescapable ethnic particularities, like Greek Orthodox
Christianity which in practice sets the Greeks apart from, say, the
Russians as well as all other Christians).

The wishy-washy, all numbers win, kind of spiritual aspiration cannot
survive long-term since it treats ethnicity, and by extension organic
hierarchy, as an impediment. It does so because it focuses on the
short-term wellness of the individual where there are no concerns for
the rigid structures that (i) enable intergenerational continuity and
(ii) keep irresponsible fellows away from items they are not prepared
to handle. This frameless individualism is an expression of caprice
masquerading as religiosity. It is unbridled want with the attendant
unaccountability. So, no, "modern happiness", as the CNN piece
suggests in its title, is not enough.

I am not religious because I do not belong to a community of the
aforementioned kind (not yet, anyway, though I am the kind of person
that makes things happen---and I am persistent). I know from personal
experience that polytheism has always persisted in Greece, sometimes
in closed circles and others through family heritage. I also am aware
that there are many groups that are public all over the Greek world. I
might, in fact, join one such group in my part of the world, if the
conditions are right. I will not do it to discuss theology, though
that might be an interesting activity on the side. I will do it to
contribute to a community.

I could become a Christian but it is not sufficiently ethnic and, at
core, remains ecumenical in its pursuits. Ethnicity is key. I do not
want the Chinese, the Germans, the Arabs, to become Greeks or vice
versa. Each culture has its own particularities. Diversity through
locality is the nice thing of our species. A person from another
cultural milieu can become Greek by adopting the Greek customs, for
example, though this practically means that such a person commits to
the community long-term through interpersonal connections.

An ethnic religion is not trying to convince others of the religion's
merits or the correctness of its theological propositions. It does not
carry out missionary work and is not promising any kind of reward to
anyone who chooses to convert to it. The ethnic group grows organically.

I have a theology, though that without a religion is what nerds do:
interesting, but not interesting enough (and, yes, this is
self-criticism). What nerds also do is get distracted by the
technicalities, such as how many gods there should be, while missing
the bigger picture, which in the case of religion is the presence of
ethnically defined community over the long-term. If you do not have a
community, and if that community does not outlive you, you do not have
a religion.

In my case, ethnicity is Greek simply because it is the one I grew up
in and can intuit about. There is nothing inherently better about
Greekness, nor is this tacit xenophobia: Greek is a state of mind and,
in fact, Xenia (Ξενία) symbolised through Xenial Zeus (Ξένιος Ζευς) is
an important ideal, which is also invoked in Homer's Odyssey, by the
way, but I digress. To appreciate ethnicity is to recognise the fact
that togetherness is stronger between people with shared cultural
associations. When Spain won the World Cup last week, millions of
Spaniards, including those who do not like football, came together as
one. Ethnicity is a practical consideration of what is the most
reliable basis for solidarity and its concomitant collectivism.

At any rate, I recognise that as a philosopher and a disciplined,
energetic person I have the talents to serve a higher purpose with
great effect. These talents are not mine to keep. They are not for my
enrichment, nor to boost my ego. I am the medium through which a
timeless message is echoed. Through my daily deeds, I accept the duty
to speak my mind in earnest; the duty to inspire people to aspire
towards excellence; the duty to be the elder brother or father figure
or faithful fellow traveller even vis-à-vis strangers, so that I lead
them where they need to be, sometimes with a gentle touch and at
others with tough love.
